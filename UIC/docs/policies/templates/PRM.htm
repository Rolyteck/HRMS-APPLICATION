Information Delivery Strategy

 

 

 

Introduction

 

The Information Delivery Strategy for an organisation should be based on the generally accepted Business Intelligence Framework as discussed in this section.

 

 

 

Business Intelligence

 

The following definitions were extracted from authoritative white papers on business intelligence:

 

         Business intelligence describes the enterprise's ability to access and explore information (often contained in a data warehouse) and to analyze that information to develop insights and understanding, which leads to improved and informed decision making [Gartner].

 

         Business Intelligence applications are decision support tools that enable real-time, interactive access, analysis and manipulation of mission-critical corporate information. These applications provide users with valuable insights into key operating information to quickly identify business problems and opportunities. Users are able to access and leverage large amounts of information to analyze relationships and understand trends that, ultimately, support business decisions. These tools prevent the potential loss of knowledge within the enterprise that results from massive information accumulation that is not readily accessible or in usable form [Cherry Tree].

 

         Business intelligence systems focus on improving the access and delivery of business information to both information providers and information consumers [IBM].

 

The Oxford Dictionary defines intelligence as intellect or understanding, and intelligential as quickness of understanding.

 

Based on iDevWorks Technologies experience in the business intelligence field and on the above definitions, iDevWorks Technologies definition of business intelligence is:

 

Business intelligence is the gathering of relevant and accurate information, that is stored to  enable rapid access for analysis purposes to improve an organisations ability to make effective decisions quickly.

 

 

Framework

 

The following Business Intelligence Framework would be applied in this project:

 

 

Information is gathered on a day-to-day basis by the organisations operational systems. Sometimes external information is sourced, e.g. price indicators, stock exchange trades. The rules and processes defining this gathering of data are of paramount importance to the success of a BI strategy.

 

This data is organised and stored in a number of ways, but always in such a way as to ensure a consistent view of the organisation across the business functions and systems to enable rapid access for reporting and analysis purposes. The management, measurement, access, availability/retrieval around the storage of data must be designed and implemented so as to meet the business requirements.

 

The organised data is then exploited by users at all levels of the business to assist in achieving the organisational objectives. The exploitation and viewing tools and the management thereof must be integral to the strategy and function of the Business Intelligence Information System.

 

 

Systems and Data Stores

 

In order to position the various business intelligence components within the framework, it is necessary to map the types of systems and data stores to the framework.

 

 

Operational Systems are the systems that are deployed within an organisation to support and/or automate steps in the business processes. They turn the wheels of the organisation and if they stop the company stops. Operational systems are characterised by:

 

         Their pre-occupation with transaction recording, They take orders, deliver goods and services, sign up new customers, enter new data, correct old data, etc.

         They almost always deal with one account at a time and information retrievals are generally lookups of single records of data that is current.

         The data is volatile (ever changing).

         There are a number of different users, at varying competency and management levels depending on the nature of the business process, recording transactions.

 

Operational Data Stores are the repositories of operational data captured through the operational systems. These repositories are structured in a way that would guarantee data integrity. The objective in the design of these repositories is to store and maintain a data item as one single instance and not to replicate it. This objective is not always achievable and it is sometimes necessary to create consolidated operational stores.

 

Data Management Systems are required to extract data from the operational data stores, consolidate it and change it. The amount of consolidation and change depends on the analytical requirements. This transformed data populates the analytical data stores. These Data Management systems can also be used to extract data from one analytical data store and populate another analytical data store.

 

Operational Data Stores are optimised to meet the requirements of the gathering process and similarly Analytical Data Stores are optimised to support the exploitation process. In these Analytical Data Stores where huge volumes of historical information need to be addressed it is essential to store it in such a way that quick responses to queries are provided. It is imperative to understand that in this environment the replication of data is managed through the consolidation and population process. Many Analytical Data Stores might be created depending on the user requirements of the exploitation systems, as well as the exploitation objectives.

 

Exploitation Systems are available to support the decision-making process in business. They can either be used to monitor and manage a known pattern or trend, or to discover new or unknown patterns or trends. These systems provide different perspectives on the business and use a number of techniques to present that to the users, such as graphical visualisation, statistical analysis, exception reporting and business scorecard measurement.

 

Temporary Data Stores are used where an Analytical Data Store, or a part of it, is copied for analysis by a user either in an off-line mode or for performance reasons.

 

 

 

Components

The following diagram positions the various business intelligence components within the business intelligence framework.

 

 

The following definitions for the above components {Kimball: 14-28}:

 

Extraction means reading and understanding the source data, and copying the parts that are needed to the Staging Area.

 

The Transformation process is the manipulation of the extracted source data to prepare it for the analytical data stores. The following steps are examples of transformation steps:

 

         Cleaning the data by correcting misspellings, resolving inconsistencies (e.g. postal code), dealing with missing data elements and parsing it into standard formats.

 

         Purging selected fields from the legacy data that are not useful for the analytical data store.

 

         Combining data sources.

 

Loading the extracted and transformed data into the target analytical data store.

 

Metadata is the data about data, describing the source data structures/layouts, the transformation process and the data structures of the analytical data stores.

 

Staging Area is a temporary storage area that is used during the extraction, transformation and loading processes to prepare data for the target analytical data store.

 

Data Warehouse is the queryable source of data in the organisation. The data warehouse is frequently updated on a controlled load basis as data is corrected, snapshots are accumulated, and statuses and labels are changed.

 

Datamart is a logical subset of a complete data warehouse.

 

Executive/Management Information Systems is a collection of tools that query, analyse and present information targeted to support a business need. These queries are usually presented as a set of pre-built templates.

 

Data Mining is a collection of tools that supports an iterative discovery process to find patterns and trends using a collection of powerful analysis techniques.

 

Query/Reporting is a specific kind of end user data access tool that invites the user to form their own queries.

 

 

Information Delivery Framework

 

 

 

Introduction

 

Driving business efficiency means providing access to information and notification of critical business developments. Delivery of information must be timely and relevant, personalised to the user. In addition, security measures must ensure that users receive only the information for which they are authorised.

 

This section highlights the components that should be considered in developing an Information Delivery Strategy.

 

 

Zachman Framework

 

The Zachman Framework can be used as a template to structure the development of the Information Delivery Strategy.

 

The Zachman Framework as it applies to organisations is simply a logical structure for classifying and organising the descriptive representations of an organisation that are significant to the management of the organisation as well as to the development of the organisations information systems.

 

 

The six interrogatives should be answered from the various perspectives. Defining the Information Delivery Strategy should address the first two perspectives, namely the Planner and Owner perspectives.

 

 

What-Information

Information must be defined to clarify the scope of the Information Delivery Strategy. Information would not only include the data recorded in the organisations databases, but could also include printed documents, and unstructured data such as emails and electronic documents.

 

         Information Charter

 

A charter should include the organisations definition of   information and the guiding principles for information delivery.

 

         Classification

 

Information should be classified in terms of business reliance/continuity, confidentiality and value.

 

         Approach

 

What is the approach of the organisation to information, e.g. the right information, to the right people at the right time? This could be a very expensive approach and cannot always be justified in terms of business value.

 

         Ownership

 

Information ownership and custodianship should be allocated for each information element.

 

 

How - Processes

 

Understanding information requires an understanding of the business processes and the information flow.

 

The following information processes should also be considered:

 

         Governance

         Acquisition

         Transformation

         Deployment

         Security

         Development

 

 

Where - Location

 

Where should the information be delivered? That will determine where the information should be stored and how it should be delivered in terms of the technology and connectivity issues.

 

 

 

Who - People

 

The various stakeholders in the organisations information should be defined as well as their use of the information, in terms of roles one individual might have more than one role in the organisation.

 

 

 

When - Time

The various stakeholders in the organisations information should be defined as well as their use of the information, in terms of roles one individual might have more than one role in the organisation.

 

 

Why - Objectives

 

The objectives for the Information Delivery Strategy must be defined and the delivery against these must be measured on an ongoing basis.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Strategy Development

 

 

 

 

Introduction

 

Developing an Information Delivery Strategy would depend on the organisations culture and maturity. This would normally be determined through a readiness assessment.

 

 

 

Capability Maturity Model

 

The Capability Maturity Model provides organisations with guidance on how to gain control of their processes for developing and maintaining software and how to evolve toward a culture of software engineering and management excellence. The CMM was designed to guide organisations in selecting process improvement strategies by determining current process maturity and identifying the few issues most critical to software quality and process improvement. By focusing on a Limited set of activities and working aggressively to achieve them, an organisation can steadily improve its organisation-wide software process to enable continuous and lasting gains in software process capability.

 

 

 

The maturity level of the organisation would determine the approach in developing the Information Delivery Strategy.

 

 

Approach

 

The Information Charter should be the foundation of the Information Delivery Strategy. This would commit the executives of the organisation to the information delivery, as well as communicate their approach to implementing information delivery.

 

Defining the first two perspectives of the Zachman Framework would provide the Information Delivery Strategy. This should be aligned to the Information Charter and would provide a framework for implementing information delivery. This can either be done top-down, based on key performance indicators, or bottom-up where information delivery is determined by operational requirements. The approach should be determined based on the organisational maturity and culture.

 

There is a tendency to design information requirements based on business processes. A different perspective should be to fit business processes around information requirements.
